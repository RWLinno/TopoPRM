\begin{abstract}
We study reinforcement fine-tuning for complex mathematical critique tasks,
where a language model must produce structured, multi-step evaluations of
student solutions. Existing reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards
(RLVR) approaches rely on outcome-only signals---checking only whether the
final judgement is correct---and ignore the \emph{topological quality} of the
intermediate reasoning process. This leads to three persistent problems:
(i)~models learn to guess correct verdicts without sound justification,
(ii)~they become overly dependent on surface-level formatting cues, and
(iii)~they generate unnecessarily long reasoning chains that waste inference
budget.

We propose \textbf{TopoPRM} (\textbf{Topo}logy-aware \textbf{P}rocess
\textbf{R}eward \textbf{M}odel), a composite verifiable reward that
augments the standard outcome reward with two topology-aware process
signals. First, a \emph{topological structure reward} extracts the directed
acyclic graph (DAG) of reasoning steps and penalises cycles, orphan nodes,
and structural incoherence. Second, a \emph{continuity reward} measures
step-to-step logical coherence via embedding similarity, discouraging
abrupt topic shifts within the reasoning chain. We integrate TopoPRM into a
Group Relative Policy Optimisation (GRPO) framework and further introduce a
\emph{reverse-KL distillation} stage that compresses the improved reasoning
behaviour from a 14B teacher into 7B and 1.5B student models.

Experiments on MATH500, GSM8K, CMATH, and GaoKao benchmarks show that
TopoPRM significantly improves both critique accuracy and reasoning
efficiency compared to outcome-only RLVR baselines, while the distilled
students retain the majority of gains at a fraction of the inference cost.
\end{abstract}
