\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

\paragraph{Reinforcement learning from human/verifiable feedback.}
RLHF~\citep{ouyang2022training} established the paradigm of aligning
language models via reward signals derived from human preferences.
DeepSeek-R1~\citep{deepseekr1} and DeepSeekMath~\citep{shao2024deepseekmath}
extended this to \emph{verifiable} rewards (RLVR), using programmatic
correctness checks for mathematical reasoning tasks. These approaches rely
exclusively on outcome-level signals and do not supervise the intermediate
reasoning process. TopoPRM complements RLVR by adding process-level
topology rewards that remain fully verifiable.

\paragraph{Process reward models.}
\citet{lightman2023lets} introduced process reward models (PRMs) that
assign scalar rewards to each reasoning step, trained via human annotations
of step-level correctness. Math-Shepherd~\citep{wang2024math} automates
PRM data collection using Monte Carlo estimation. While PRMs provide
step-level supervision, they require either expensive human labels or
learned verifiers that may themselves be unreliable. In contrast, TopoPRM
derives process rewards from structural properties of the reasoning graph,
requiring no learned components.

\paragraph{Graph-structured reasoning.}
Graph-of-Thought~\citep{besta2024graph} and GRP~\citep{grp2025} model
reasoning as graph construction, enabling non-linear exploration of
solution paths. GRP is most closely related to our work in that it also
considers graph structure during training. However, GRP focuses on
\emph{generating} graph-structured solutions, whereas TopoPRM uses graph
topology as a \emph{reward signal} for reinforcement fine-tuning of
standard autoregressive critique generation. This distinction means
TopoPRM does not require architectural modifications and can be applied
to any pretrained language model.

\paragraph{Reasoning compression and distillation.}
Recent work has explored compressing verbose chain-of-thought reasoning
into shorter but equally accurate outputs~\citep{reasoning_compression_2025}.
Knowledge distillation from large teacher models to smaller
students~\citep{hinton2015distilling} is a well-established technique.
